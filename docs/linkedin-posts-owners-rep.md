## Post 1 — The most common first call

The most common first call I get goes like this.

"We've found a yacht we like. The broker is helpful. The yard wants 35 percent up front. Are we ready?"

The honest answer is almost always: not yet, and you need someone in the room who isn't paid by the seller, the yard, or the broker.

That role is a yacht owner's representative. The job is to act in your interest, end to end, on a fee that doesn't move when the price moves.

A few things a good owner's rep does in the first month:

- Reviews the contract before you sign, not after
- Validates the spec against the use case you described, not the one the yard prefers
- Builds the payment schedule against verifiable milestones
- Flags warranty and arbitration clauses that quietly favour the yard

If you're at the stage where you're being asked to commit money, the cheapest insurance you can buy is an independent set of eyes that gets paid the same regardless of what you decide.

## Post 2 — What an owner's rep actually does

There is still a lot of confusion about what a yacht owner's representative does in a new build.

Here is the rough shape of the first six months.

Week 1 to 2: yard shortlist. We assess capacity, current order book, recent delivery quality, and financial health. Three or four yards get a serious look. The rest get a polite no.

Week 3 to 6: spec lock. The owner has a brief. The yard has a template. The job is to bridge the two without letting either side dictate. Every line item gets challenged on cost, weight, maintenance, and resale.

Week 6 to 10: contract. Payment schedule against verifiable milestones, not calendar dates. Warranty terms that bite. Arbitration in a jurisdiction the owner can actually use.

Week 10 onward: build supervision. On-site presence at major milestones. Independent class and flag liaison. Defects log maintained by us, not the yard.

The fee doesn't move with the build cost. That is the entire point.

If you're considering a 24 to 60 metre new build and want a second opinion on the structure of the deal, the team at Foreland Marine does this for a living. https://forelandmarine.com/owners-representation

## Post 3 — The independence problem

A short note on independence in yacht owner's representation.

Most people who present themselves as owner's reps in this industry have one of these on the side:

- A brokerage arm that earns commission on the sale
- A yard relationship that pays referral fees
- A management contract that activates the moment the yacht splashes
- A favoured naval architect or designer who returns the favour

None of these are illegal. All of them quietly bend the advice you receive.

The test we apply to ourselves at Foreland Marine is simple. If a recommendation we make would cost us money, would we still make it? If the answer is no, we have a problem.

This is why the firm takes no commissions from yards, brokers, designers, or suppliers. The fee is paid by the owner, scoped at the start, and doesn't move when the budget moves.

If you're at the early stage of buying or building, ask any prospective owner's rep this one question: "What other revenue do you earn from this transaction?" The answer tells you everything.

## Post 4 — Three roles people conflate

Three roles that get conflated in superyacht buying. They are not the same thing.

Broker. Finds the yacht or the yard. Paid by commission, usually 5 to 10 percent of the deal. Their incentive is to close. That can align with the buyer. It can also not.

Project manager. Runs the build or refit on the ground. Often employed by the yard or the contractor. Their incentive is to deliver on the contract that exists, not to challenge whether the contract is the right one.

Owner's representative. Sits on the buyer's side of the table from the start. Reviews the deal before it's signed. Supervises the build against the deal. Handles the warranty period. Paid by the owner on a fixed scope, no commissions.

You can buy a yacht with only a broker. People do. The cost of doing so usually shows up two years later, in disputes the contract didn't anticipate.
