What a well-run refit looks like

Part 4 of a five-part series on the decisions that shape a first owner's experience.

A refit is one of the more rewarding undertakings in a yacht's life. Done well, it returns the yacht close to delivery condition while bringing it forward to current standards in safety, technology, and comfort. The yacht emerges with another decade or more of confident service ahead of it.

The difference between a refit that goes well and one that struggles is rarely about the yard's competence. The major centres, Palma, Barcelona, La Ciotat, Genoa, Viareggio, and the northern European halls, are all world-class. What separates a smooth project from a difficult one is how the work is set up before the yacht enters the shed.


A refit is a programme, not a list of jobs

The most useful shift is to treat the refit as a programme. A list of jobs invites change orders, scope drift, and surprises. A programme has a structure: a defined scope, a sequence, a budget, a contingency, and a closeout.

Inside the programme there can still be hundreds of individual items. The programme holds them together, so each one fits with the others and the owner has a clear view of cost and time at every stage.


Scope definition

The first piece of work is scope. A good scope is anchored in current yard rates and material prices, and in an honest survey of the yacht's actual condition. Older yachts in particular reveal more on inspection than on paper. The scope should reflect the yacht as it is, not as the original specification suggested it should be.


Yard selection

The right yard depends on the work. A complex carbon mast refit suits one set of yards. A long-range motor yacht with extensive systems work suits another. The yard's current workload, capacity in the relevant disciplines, and quality of senior project management matter as much as the headline rates. A yard at 80 percent capacity will run the project differently from a yard fighting to fill the dock.


Contract structure

A clear refit contract distinguishes between fixed-price elements, day-rate elements, and materials-on-cost elements. Each has its place. A contract that fixes prices on work that cannot reasonably be priced in advance creates pressure that surfaces as disputes. A contract that runs work on day rates where a fixed price was achievable forfeits the certainty the owner was entitled to.


Contingency

A reasonable contingency on a major refit sits in the range of ten to twenty percent of the contract value, more on older yachts or where access is restricted. Contingency is not money to be spent. It is money set aside against the things a refit reveals once the yacht is opened up. The best owners and the best yards treat contingency as a shared discipline.


Classification timing

A refit should align with the yacht's classification survey cycle wherever the timing allows. A five-year special survey conducted alongside the refit is the most efficient use of the yacht's time out of the water. The classification society, the flag, and the yard need to agree on this from the planning stage, not from the day the yacht arrives.


Reporting

Weekly progress and cost reports keep the project visible. They should show planned versus actual on time and on cost, with any change orders opened or closed during the week. An owner reading the report should be able to know, in five minutes, whether the project is on track.


The closeout

A refit ends with a structured handover, a defects list, a documented set of works, and warranty terms on the new equipment and systems. The yacht should leave the yard with everything in writing, ready for the seasons ahead.


What the owner gets

When the programme runs well, the yacht emerges on time, near budget, and to a quality standard the owner can verify. The crew is back aboard a yacht they recognise, with the systems they need and a clear record of what has been done. The work protects the seasons that follow.
