What a well-run refit looks like

Part 4 of a five-part series on the decisions that shape a first owner's experience.

A refit is one of the more rewarding pieces of work in a yacht's life. Done well, a refit returns the yacht close to delivery condition while bringing it forward to current standards in safety, technology, and comfort. Done well, the yacht emerges with another decade or more of confident service ahead of it.

The difference between a refit that goes well and a refit that struggles is rarely about the yard's competence. The yards in the major refit centres of Palma, Barcelona, La Ciotat, Genoa, Viareggio, and the northern European refit halls are world-class. The difference is in how the project is set up before the yacht enters the shed.


A refit is a programme, not a list of jobs

The most useful shift in mindset is to treat the refit as a programme. A list of jobs invites change orders, scope drift, and surprises. A programme has a structure: a defined scope, a sequence, a budget, a contingency, and a closeout.

Inside the programme there can still be hundreds of individual items. The programme is what holds them together so that each one fits with the others, and so that the owner has a clear view of cost and time at every stage.


Scope, anchored in current rates

The first piece of work is scope definition. A good refit scope is anchored in current yard rates, current material prices, and an honest survey of the yacht's actual condition. Older yachts in particular reveal more on inspection than on paper. The scope should reflect the yacht as it is, not as the original specification suggested it should be.


Yard selection

The right refit yard depends on the work. A complex carbon mast refit has natural homes. A long-range motor yacht with extensive systems work has different ones. The yard's current workload, capacity in the relevant disciplines, and quality of senior project management matter as much as the headline rates. A yard at 80 percent capacity will run the project differently from a yard fighting to fill the dock.


Contract structure

A clear refit contract distinguishes between the fixed-price elements, the day-rate elements, and the materials-on-cost elements. Each has its place. A contract that tries to fix-price work that cannot reasonably be fixed-priced creates pressure that surfaces as disputes. A contract that day-rates work that could have been fixed-priced loses the owner the certainty they were entitled to.


Contingency

A reasonable contingency on a major refit sits in the range of ten to twenty percent of the contract value, more on older yachts or where access is restricted. Contingency is not money to be spent. It is money set aside against the things a refit reveals once the yacht is opened up. Good owners and good yards both treat contingency as a shared discipline.


Classification timing

Where possible, a refit aligns with the yacht's classification survey cycle. A five-year special survey conducted alongside the refit is the most efficient use of the yacht's time out of the water. The classification society, the flag, and the yard need to be aligned on this from the planning stage, not from the day the yacht arrives.


Reporting

Weekly progress and cost reports keep the project visible. The reports should show planned versus actual on time, planned versus actual on cost, and any change orders open or closed during the week. An owner reading the report should be able to know, in five minutes, whether the project is on track.


The closeout

A refit ends with a structured handover, a defects list, a documented set of works, and warranty terms on the new equipment and systems. The yacht should leave the yard with everything in writing, ready for the next phase of operation.


What the owner gets

When the programme runs well, the yacht emerges on time, near budget, and to a quality standard the owner can verify. The crew is back aboard a yacht they recognise, with the systems they need, and a clear record of what has been done. The next several seasons are protected by the work.
