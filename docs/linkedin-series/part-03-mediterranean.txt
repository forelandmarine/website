Cruising the Mediterranean with confidence

Part 3 of a five-part series on the decisions that shape a first owner's experience.

The Mediterranean is the centre of the global yacht season. From late spring through early autumn, the Western Med from the Balearics through the Côte d'Azur, the Italian Riviera, and the Adriatic carries a remarkable concentration of the world fleet. For a first-time owner, cruising the Med is one of the most rewarding parts of ownership, and one of the most rewarding to plan well.

The framework that governs EU waters is straightforward once it is understood. A short hour spent on it before the yacht is delivered makes the difference between an owner who moves freely and an owner who is constantly working around constraints set up at the wrong time.


VAT-paid status

A yacht with VAT-paid status in the EU can move freely within EU waters indefinitely. The yacht is treated, from the perspective of customs and VAT, as goods already in free circulation. For most owners who plan extensive EU cruising, VAT-paid status is the cleanest position to hold.

VAT can be paid on import, paid on first sale within the EU, or accounted for through specific national regimes where the yacht qualifies. Each route has its place. The route that fits a particular owner depends on the ownership structure, the flag, the intended use, and the country of import.


Temporary admission

A non-EU yacht owned by a non-EU resident can use temporary admission, which permits up to eighteen months of EU waters time without paying EU VAT. Temporary admission suits owners who genuinely cruise globally, and whose Mediterranean time is one season among several. It is less suited to owners who plan to base the yacht in the EU year-round.


Chartering from EU ports

Chartering the yacht from an EU port has its own framework. EU VAT applies to the charter fee, with the rate and base depending on where the charter starts and the routing of the trip. The set-up of the owning entity and the charter company matters here, and it pays to design these before the yacht is delivered, not after.


The Spanish refit advantage

Spain operates an Inward Processing Relief regime that allows non-EU yachts to refit in Spanish yards without paying EU VAT on the works. For a five million euro refit, the relief is worth roughly one million euro. The result is that the major Spanish yards, in particular those in the Balearics and on the mainland Mediterranean coast, are highly competitive for non-EU yachts considering refit work.

The UK is currently working through similar arrangements, and the picture there will evolve over the coming years. For now, an owner planning a major refit on a non-EU-flagged yacht should know that Spain offers a meaningful structural advantage.


The practical setup

A first-time owner cruising the Med should typically have answers to four questions before delivery.

The VAT status of the yacht and how that will hold up across the first three years.

Whether charter is part of the plan, and if so, how the owning structure supports it.

Where the yacht will be based for refit and winter work, and how the chosen yard's tax position interacts with the yacht's flag.

How the EU waters limit, where temporary admission applies, will be tracked across years.

None of this is difficult. It is, however, much easier to set up correctly at the start than to restructure later. A yacht in a clean position from delivery enjoys the Med freely. A yacht in a messy position spends part of every season working around earlier decisions.
