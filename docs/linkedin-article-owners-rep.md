## What a Yacht Owner's Representative Actually Does (and What It Costs)

A friend of mine recently asked me, over dinner, what it is I actually do. He had just been pitched on a new build and wanted to know whether the role of "owner's representative" was something he needed, or whether it was a service the broker, the yard, or the management company would already provide.

It was a fair question. The phrase "yacht owner's representative" gets used loosely in this industry. It appears in pitch decks from brokerages, marketing material from yards, and the service descriptions of management companies whose income depends on the answer being "yes, we can do that for you, and we will roll it into your existing fee."

The role exists for a reason. The reason is that none of those people work for the owner.

This is what a yacht owner's representative actually does, what it costs, when you need one, and the questions you should ask before you appoint anyone to that role.

### The role in one sentence

A yacht owner's representative is an independent professional retained by the owner to oversee a new build or major refit, from concept through to the end of the warranty period, with no commercial relationship with any other party in the project.

The two operative words are "independent" and "owner." Strip either out and the role collapses into something else.

### What independence means in this industry

Most firms presenting themselves as yacht owner's representatives have at least one of the following on the side:

- A brokerage arm that earns commission on the sale.
- A yard relationship that pays referral fees.
- A management contract that activates the moment the yacht splashes.
- A preferred-supplier list that returns the favour through equipment specifications.
- A favoured naval architect, designer, or surveyor whose engagement triggers a quiet kickback.

None of these are illegal. All of them quietly bend the advice the owner receives.

The simple test is this. If a recommendation we make would cost us money, would we still make it? If the answer is no, the firm is selling owner's representation under a misleading label. The advice the owner gets in those arrangements may still be technically correct. It is not free of pressure.

A genuinely independent yacht owner's representative earns income only from the owner. There is no adjacent business that benefits when the owner spends more, changes yacht, or places the vessel on the charter market. The fee is fixed at the start of the engagement and does not move with the build cost.

### The five jobs in a new build

In a new build, the role splits into five phases.

**One: yard selection.** A serious shortlist of three to five shipyards, based on capability, current order book, recent delivery quality, capacity for the planned start date, and financial health. The choice of yard is the single biggest determinant of cost, quality, and timeline. It is also the phase where commission-based "advisors" do the most damage, because the yard that pays the highest referral fee is rarely the right yard for the project.

**Two: contract negotiation.** A detailed review of the build contract before signature. Payment schedule tied to verifiable milestones, not calendar dates. Liquidated damages that bite. Warranty terms that survive delivery. Arbitration in a jurisdiction the owner can actually use. Specifications cross-checked against the brief, line by line. Most of the value of owner's representation lives in this phase, before any money has changed hands.

**Three: build supervision.** On-site presence at major milestones throughout the build period. Attendance at production and design meetings. Independent quality inspections. Milestone payment verification. Defects log maintained by the owner's team, not the yard. Structured monthly reporting so the owner is informed without having to live at the yard.

**Four: sea trials and delivery.** Coordination of harbour and sea trials, commissioning of every onboard system, snag list management, handover documentation, and formal acceptance on behalf of the owner. The acceptance signature is the moment the owner takes legal title. It should not be given lightly, and it should not be given to systems that have not been demonstrated to perform to specification.

**Five: warranty period.** Continued involvement for the first twelve to twenty-four months after delivery. Defect tracking, warranty claim management, and continued liaison with the shipyard until every contractual obligation is closed. The warranty period is when most disputes arise, and where independent representation pays for itself many times over.

### The five jobs in a refit

In a refit, the structure compresses but the principles are the same.

One: scope definition and budget, anchored in current yard rates and material prices, with the owner told the truth about what each item costs before any contract is signed.

Two: yard selection, based on capacity for the planned start window, capability in the relevant disciplines, current workload, and commercial terms.

Three: refit project management on the ground, with subcontractor coordination, class and flag liaison, change order discipline, and weekly progress and cost reporting.

Four: quality and acceptance, with independent inspections at each milestone and a structured handover process.

Five: cost and schedule control, with continuous tracking against budget and forecast updates so overruns are visible long before the final invoice.

The fee for refit representation does not move with the cost of the refit. That is deliberate. It is the only structure that aligns the representative's incentives with the owner's.

### What it costs

The rule of thumb across reputable independent firms is consistent.

Full new build representation typically runs at one to three percent of the contract value, scoped at the start of the engagement and not linked to the final build cost. A 40-metre new build at 30 million euro contract value sits in the lower end of that range. A complex performance sailing yacht with extensive bespoke engineering, or a project that runs to four or five years, sits at the upper end.

Refit representation is usually a fixed monthly fee against the agreed project duration, in the range of fifteen to forty thousand euro per month for a major refit, depending on the scope and the on-site presence required.

Two structures should give an owner pause. The first is any percentage-of-final-cost fee: the representative is then quietly rewarded when costs grow, which is the opposite of the alignment the owner is paying for. The second is a fee bundled into a wider package that includes brokerage, charter management, or yard introduction commissions: in that arrangement the representation fee is functionally subsidised by adjacent revenue streams the owner cannot see and cannot control.

The cleanest structure is a fixed-scope fee, paid by the owner, agreed at the start, with a defined process for any change of scope. It is the simplest expression of the only alignment that matters: the representative makes more money by saving the owner more money.

### When to hire one

The most effective time to engage an owner's representative is as early as possible, ideally before signing a Letter of Intent or shortlisting yards. The largest single contribution the role makes is in the contract and yard-selection phase, before any money has changed hands.

It is, however, never too late. Mid-build engagements are routine. Late engagements before sea trials are routine. The work is harder when the contract is already locked in, but it is far better than continuing without representation through delivery and into the warranty period.

### When you can probably get away without one

Below 24 metres the technical and contractual complexity may not justify full owner's representation, although a contract review and yard-selection support can still be valuable. Standard production yachts from established builders, on standard contract terms, can usually be bought without dedicated representation.

Above 24 metres, and particularly on bespoke new builds or significant refits, the case for independent representation is strong. Above 40 metres it is overwhelming. The cost of getting the contract or the yard wrong, on a project of that scale, is several multiples of the cost of the representation itself.

### Five questions to ask any prospective owner's representative

Whether the candidate is Foreland Marine or anyone else, these five questions surface most of what an owner needs to know before signing an engagement letter.

**One: what other revenue do you earn from this transaction?** If the answer involves yard commissions, broker referral fees, supplier kickbacks, or a management contract that activates on delivery, the advice you receive is not independent.

**Two: are you SYBAss accredited and on the YORR register?** These are the recognised standards in the superyacht industry. They confirm the firm has been vetted against an industry benchmark and operates under a code of conduct.

**Three: who on your team will actually be on-site at the yard?** Names, CVs, presence model in writing. The person you meet at the pitch is sometimes not the person who runs the project.

**Four: how is your fee structured?** A fixed-scope fee, owner-paid, that does not move with the build cost is the only structure that aligns incentives properly.

**Five: can I speak to the owners of three recent projects you have represented?** Direct reference calls are the single best test of quality. Any owner's representative worth engaging will provide them without hesitation.

### Closing

An owner's representative is the cheapest insurance an owner can buy at the start of a new build or major refit. The role only works if it is structurally independent, owner-paid, and accountable to nobody else in the project. Anything else is a service in name only.
